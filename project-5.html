Part.01
Initial Research and Experimentation
1. Research on papers is crucial! Crucial! Crucial!
Quickly grasp information through rough reading, observing the general picture without going into too much detail. Start your research with top conferences (CVPR, ICCV, ECCV, NIPS, IJCAI, AAAI, MICCAI, etc.) and journals (TPAMI, IJCV, TMI, MIA, etc.). Organize keywords, search all related articles from the past five years, and then meticulously select them based on team, task, year, and similarity. Next, engage in thorough reading of the articles. Requirements of thorough reading include:
1) Understanding the core innovative points of the article; 2) Identifying its flaws;
3) Recognizing other related articles and their relationships;
4) Repeating steps 1, 2, and 3 for these articles;
5) Forming a detailed logical network, abstracting their common ideas and differences, and identifying their flaws.
2. How to quickly get into your topic? Reading papers? No! Replicating experiments? Yes!
Start with open-source codes from top conferences and journals. Run the codes first, then understand them. Understand each module of the engineering code as a black box, knowing its input, output, and purpose. Then, delve into the internal details of the black box, ensuring that the technology within can be utilized. Reading papers supports innovation with theoretical backing, for substantial discussion.
Running codes helps to ground innovation, to test your idea.
Combining both can greatly correct one-sided understanding of literature and simplify the complex abstract logic in codes.
3. Technological innovation is built on comprehensive and detailed paper research and profound code understanding.
Innovation originates from your understanding and insight into the current field's technical thoughts (both within the same task and across different tasks). Summarize others' work advantages and flaws; abstract their common thoughts; identify their blind spots (in thought and technology).
Innovative points are relative to current field technology, so any innovation proposed without thoroughly researching all techniques might already exist. Innovation partly comes from creative ideas in natural science (unrelated to the field), and partly from insight and integration of technology and thoughts (related to the field). For instance, many innovations in the design of cars, airplanes, and other machines need to refer to biological morphology. Proposing innovation requires divergent thinking based on real problems and clever solutions using other ideas. Implementing and testing innovation requires in-depth understanding of the field's work and proficient mastery of technology.
4. Adjustment and optimization of technological innovation points are based on comparative and ablation experiments.
The work of experimentation includes implementing technological innovations and further verifying and adjusting them. The effectiveness of each module needs to be validated through ablation experiments.

Part.02
Writing the Paper
1. Abstract and Introduction are of utmost importance.
Comparatively, the Introduction is even more important. The first step is to clarify the writing direction of the intro, which essentially sets the tone of the article.
1) Clarify the selling points of our work (core technical points, core innovative points).
2) Establish our stance.
This is the most challenging and skill-demanding part.
2.1) Identify others' targets (blind spots).
This is why research on papers is so important.
2.2) Hence, the establishment of innovative points and others' target points are interconnected.
In research, I believe: Core innovative points are often fixed and must withstand comparison with SOTA models.
In my view, establishing targets is even more important than innovative points (Read related literature with your core ideas, compare, associate, abstract, integrate). Hitting the target with innovative points makes the narrative logical and easy to present.
Targets are like the beams of a house; if not properly established, the structure will be unstable and vulnerable.
3. Once targets are established and correlated with innovative points, what remains is narration.
3.1) Arrange the overall logic, design the function of each paragraph. Principle: Logic should progress from simple to complex, serving to introduce your core ideas.
3.2) Pay special attention to logical connections between paragraphs. Transitions should avoid abruptness.
3.3) The beginning of each paragraph should be the main point. Avoid lengthy narrative setups; instead, express viewpoints directly and incisively.
4. Avoid verbose and redundant wording, and long, complex sentences. Example: "based on WSIs at a single resolution" vs. "based on single resolution WSIs."
5. Avoid excessive use of adjectives.
Refrain from using subjective terms to evaluate others' and your own work.
6. Be objective when critiquing targets, stating facts, blind spots, and issues.
7. Use verbs carefully (propose, design, develop, introduce, utilize, leverage).
